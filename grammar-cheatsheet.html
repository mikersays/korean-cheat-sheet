Korean Grammar Cheat Sheet, 한국어 문법

A structural reference for building Korean sentences — particles, conjugation, connectors, and the patterns that hold them together. Can't read Hangul yet? Start with the Hangul Reading Guide first.

00 Foundations · 기초

Read this once. Every later section uses these conventions.

Hangul syllable structure & 받침

A Hangul syllable block has an initial consonant, a vowel, and an optional final consonant. That final consonant is called the 받침 ("supporting floor").

가  =  ㄱ + ㅏ           → no 받침 (vowel-final)
강  =  ㄱ + ㅏ + ㅇ      → has 받침 (consonant-final)
책  =  ㅊ + ㅐ + ㄱ      → has 받침
사과  =  사 (no 받침) + 과 (no 받침)
학생  =  학 (받침) + 생 (받침)

Korean particles and endings come in two shapes chosen by whether the preceding syllable has a 받침. Same meaning, smoother sound — consonants don't like meeting consonants, vowels don't like meeting vowels.

Notation used throughout this sheet

NotationMeans
A / BPick one based on the 받침 rule. The section will state which goes with which. Example: 은/는, 이/가, 을/를.
(으)The cushion vowel. Insert 으 only when the stem ends in a consonant. Example: 먹+(으)면 → 먹으면; 가+(으)면 → 가면.
(스), (이)Same idea, different cushion: 스 in ~(스)ㅂ니다 appears after consonants; 이 in ~(이)에요 / ~(이)라고 appears after consonants.
~"Attach here." Marks where the ending hooks onto a stem or noun. ~아요 means it attaches as stem + 아요.
The cushion rule, in one line: Korean inserts a tiny vowel (으, 스, 이) when a consonant-final word would otherwise crash into a consonant-starting ending. That's all (으), (스), (이) mean.

Stems & verb types

Every Korean verb and adjective is listed in the dictionary as stem + 다. Drop the 다 to get the stem; everything else hooks onto the stem.

가다   → stem 가-      (to go)
먹다   → stem 먹-      (to eat)
예쁘다 → stem 예쁘-    (to be pretty)

Korean has two verb types, both conjugating the same way:

  • Action verbs (동사) — do something: 가다, 먹다, 보다.
  • Descriptive verbs (형용사) — describe something. These are what English calls "adjectives," but they conjugate as verbs and need no copula: 예쁘다 → 예뻐요 ("is pretty"), not 예쁘 + 이다.

The split matters in a few places (modifier endings, plain-form declarative). Sections will flag which type a rule applies to.

What you should already know

  • How to read 한글 (Hangul) — this sheet has no romanization.
  • Roughly which jamo are vowels (ㅏ ㅑ ㅓ ㅕ ㅗ ㅛ ㅜ ㅠ ㅡ ㅣ + diphthongs) and which are consonants.
  • That syllable blocks read top-left → top-right → bottom.

01 Word Order — SOV

Korean is Subject – Object – Verb. The verb always ends the sentence.

저는    사과를    먹어요.
[주어]  [목적어]   [동사]
나      apple      eat
  • Modifiers come before what they modify (adj before noun, adv before verb).
  • Particles attach to the noun/phrase, not the word that follows.
  • Subjects and objects are routinely dropped when context is clear.

02 Particles 조사

Particles glue grammar to nouns. They are chosen based on whether the noun ends in a vowel or a consonant (받침). Same meaning, smoother sound.

Topic — 은 / 는

  • Marks the topic ("as for X").
  • 받침 → 은, no 받침 → 는 — smoother sound.
  • Used for contrast and to introduce new topics.
저는 학생이에요.   (As for me, I'm a student.)
김치는 매워요.    (Kimchi, [it] is spicy.)

Subject — 이 / 가

  • Marks the grammatical subject; emphasizes "who/what" did it.
  • 받침 → 이, no 받침 → 가 — avoids consonant crowding.
누가 왔어요?   —   민수가 왔어요.   (Who came? — Minsu came.)
은/는 vs 이/가: 은/는 = topic / contrast / general statement. 이/가 = new info / specific identification / answers "who?".

Object — 을 / 를

  • Marks the direct object.
  • 받침 → 을, no 받침 → 를 — avoids consonant crowding.
책을 읽어요.    물을 마셔요.    영화를 봐요.
(read a book.    drink water.    watch a movie.)

Possessive / "of" — 의

저의 책 → 제 책   (my book)
친구의 집           (friend's house)

Location / Time — 에

Static location, destination, time.

학교에 가요.   (going to school)
3시에 만나요. (meet at 3)
집에 있어요.  (am at home)

Action location — 에서

Where an action happens, or "from".

도서관에서 공부해요.   (study at the library)
한국에서 왔어요.       (came from Korea)

To a person — 에게 / 한테 / 께

에게 (neutral, slightly formal), 한테 (colloquial), 께 (honorific). Both 에게 and 한테 are common in speech.

친구에게 선물을 줬어요.   (I gave a gift to a friend.)
선생님께 말씀드렸어요.    (I spoke [humbly] to the teacher.)

From a person — 에게서 / 한테서

엄마한테서 편지를 받았어요.   (I got a letter from mom.)

With / and — 와/과, 하고, (이)랑

  • 와/과 (formal): 받침 → 과, no 받침 → 와 — smoother sound.
  • 하고 (neutral), (이)랑 (casual).
빵과 우유   /   빵하고 우유   /   빵이랑 우유   (bread and milk)

Also / too — 도

Replaces 은/는/이/가/을/를. Never combine with them.

저도 가요.   (I'm going too.)

Only — 만

물만 마셔요.   (I drink only water.)

From ~ to — 에서 ~ 까지 (place), 부터 ~ 까지 (time)

서울에서 부산까지   (from Seoul to Busan)
9시부터 6시까지     (from 9 to 6)

By means of / with — (으)로

받침 → 으로, no 받침/ㄹ → 로. Tool, method, direction. 으 cushions consonant endings.

젓가락으로 먹어요.   (eat with chopsticks)
한국어로 말해요.    (speak in Korean)
학교로 가요.        (head toward school)

03 The Copula 이다 / 아니다

  • 이다 = "to be (X)". No 받침 → ~예요 / 받침 → ~이에요. 이 cushions consonant endings.
  • 아니다 = "to not be (X)". Noun takes 이/가.
저는 의사예요.            (I am a doctor.)
저는 학생이에요.          (I am a student.)
저는 학생이 아니에요.     (I am not a student.)

존재 (existence): 있다 / 없다 = "there is / isn't", "have / don't have".

시간이 있어요.   돈이 없어요.   (I have time.    I have no money.)

04 Verb / Adjective Stems

Drop 다 from the dictionary form to get the stem.

가다 → 가-     먹다 → 먹-     예쁘다 → 예쁘-
(to go)        (to eat)       (to be pretty)

Korean adjectives are descriptive verbs — they conjugate just like verbs, no copula needed.

하늘이 파래요.   (The sky is blue.)   ← 파랗다 conjugated directly

06 Present Tense — 아/어/해요

Pick by the stem's last vowel.

Stem vowelEndingExample
ㅏ, ㅗ~아요살다 → 살아요
Other vowels~어요먹다 → 먹어요
하- verbs해요공부하다 → 공부해요

Vowel contractions (when stem ends in a vowel)

가 + 아요 → 가요          오 + 아요 → 와요
보 + 아요 → 봐요          주 + 어요 → 줘요
마시 + 어요 → 마셔요      서 + 어요 → 서요
배우 + 어요 → 배워요      되 + 어요 → 돼요

For formal: stem + ㅂ니다 (vowel-ending) / 습니다 (consonant-ending). 스 cushions consonant stems.

가다 → 갑니다     먹다 → 먹습니다
Caveat: roughly 10% of common verbs are irregular — applying the rule above to them produces wrong forms (e.g. 듣다 → ✗듣어요 / ✓들어요; 예쁘다 → ✗예쁘어요 / ✓예뻐요; 춥다 → ✗춥어요 / ✓추워요). See §20 Irregular Verb Stems for the families.

07 Past Tense — 았/었/했어요

Same vowel rule, plus 었.

가다 → 갔어요         먹다 → 먹었어요
하다 → 했어요         보다 → 봤어요
공부하다 → 공부했어요

Formal past: ~았/었습니다.

08 Future / Probability

~(으)ㄹ 거예요 — plain future / guess

받침 → 을 거예요, no 받침 → ㄹ 거예요. 으 cushions consonant stems.

내일 갈 거예요.       (I will go tomorrow.)
비가 올 거예요.       (It will probably rain.)

~겠~ — intention / strong assumption / politeness

제가 하겠습니다.       (I will do it.)
맛있겠어요!            (Looks delicious!)
알겠어요.              (Got it.)

~(으)려고 하다 — plan to

한국에 가려고 해요.    (I'm planning to go to Korea.)

10 Honorifics — ~(으)시~

Insert 시 into the verb stem to honor the subject.

가다 → 가시다 → 가세요 / 가십니다   (go → goes [hon.])
먹다 → 드시다 (special)               (eat → eats [hon.])
자다 → 주무시다 (special)             (sleep → sleeps [hon.])
있다 → 계시다 (special, "to be present")

Honorific particles: 께서 (subject), 께 (to). Honorific noun forms: 진지 (밥), 댁 (집), 연세 (나이), 성함 (이름), 말씀 (말).

Humble forms (lower yourself): 드리다 (주다), 뵙다 (보다), 여쭙다 (묻다).

MeaningPatternExample

or~거나 / 아니면영화 보거나 책 읽어요.I watch movies or read books.
~서 vs ~(으)니까 (because): 서 = objective cause; cannot be used with imperatives/suggestions. 니까 = subjective reason; required with 명령/청유 ("let's", "do this").

~아/어서 — "and-then" vs "because": if the second clause is an action that physically requires the first (학교에 가서 공부해요 = go to school and there study), it's sequential — same subject required. If the second is a consequence/judgment (추워서 안 가요), it's reason.

~(으)러 vs ~(으)려고 (in order to): ~(으)러 requires a motion verb (가다 / 오다 / 다니다) in the main clause: 밥 먹으러 가요. ~(으)려고 = "with the intent of" — works with any main verb: 한국에 가려고 돈을 모아요.

~는데 — four flavors: (1) contrast: 비싼데 좋아요 ("expensive but good"); (2) background: 밥 먹는데 전화 왔어요 ("I was eating when…"); (3) soft observation as ~는데요; (4) leading question setup: 시간 있는데 영화 볼래요? ("[I/you] have time, want to see a movie?").

Aspect — ~고 있다 vs ~아/어 있다

Both translate "is -ing" but mean very different things. This is the single biggest aspect trap in Korean.

FormMeaningExample
~고 있다Progressive — in the act of doing먹고 있어요 (am eating)
~아/어 있다Resultative — in the state resulting from앉아 있어요 (am seated, having sat)
 
문이 닫히고 있어요.   (The door is closing — in the act.)
문이 닫혀 있어요.     (The door is closed — and stays so.)

~아/어 있다 only attaches to intransitive verbs that produce a lasting state (앉다, 서다, 눕다, 닫히다, 열리다, 떨어지다). Transitive actions take ~고 있다.

15 Quoting & Reported Speech

Direct: ~(이)라고 하다. No space before 라고.

"가요"라고 했어요.

Indirect (contractions are normal in speech). Verb statements take ~ㄴ다고 after vowel-final stems and ~는다고 after consonant-final stems.

TypeStatementQuestionSuggestionCommand
Verb~ㄴ다고 / ~는다고~냐고~자고~(으)라고
Adjective~다고~냐고——
이다~(이)라고~(이)냐고——

Older / literary forms ~(으)냐고 for adjectives and ~느냐고 for verbs still appear in print.

간다고 했어요.        (said [he] goes)
가냐고 물었어요.      (asked if [he] goes)
가자고 했어요.        (said let's go)
가라고 했어요.        (told [him] to go)

Common contractions: ~다고 해요 → ~대요, ~(이)라고 해요 → ~(이)래요, ~냐고 해요 → ~냬요, ~자고 해요 → ~재요, ~(으)라고 해요 → ~(으)래요.

19 Causative & Passive

Korean has both lexical (built-in derived) and productive (compositional) forms. Lexical forms are by far the most common in everyday speech and must be memorized per verb.

Causative — lexical ~이/히/리/기/우/구/추~; productive ~게 하다

Lexical — many common verbs have a built-in causative.

먹다  → 먹이다     (feed)            아이에게 밥을 먹여요.
입다  → 입히다     (dress someone)   아이에게 옷을 입혀요.
앉다  → 앉히다     (seat someone)    손님을 앉혔어요.
살다  → 살리다     (save / spare)
일어나다 → 일으키다 (raise / wake)

Productive — for any verb without a lexical causative, use ~게 하다.

아이를 자게 했어요.   (I made the child sleep.)

Passive — lexical ~이/히/리/기~; productive ~아/어지다

Lexical — many transitive verbs have a paired passive form.

열다  → 열리다     (be opened)     문이 열렸어요.
닫다  → 닫히다     (be closed)     문이 닫혔어요.
보다  → 보이다     (be visible)    산이 보여요.
듣다  → 들리다     (be audible)    음악이 들려요.

Productive — ~아/어지다 turns adjectives into "become X" and some verbs into passives.

컵이 깨졌어요.        (The cup broke.)        ← 깨다 → 깨지다
날씨가 추워졌어요.    (The weather got cold.) ← 춥다 + ~아/어지다

Regular ㅂ/ㄷ/ㅅ verbs exist (입다 → 입어요, 받다 → 받아요, 웃다 → 웃어요) — irregularity is per-verb, must be learned.

21 Building a Sentence — the recipe

[Time]  [Topic은/는]  [Subject이/가]  [Place에/에서]  [Indirect obj에게]  [Object을/를]  [Adverb]  [Verb]
어제    저는                          학교에서                              친구를      오래       기다렸어요.
(Yesterday, [as for me], at school, [I] waited a long time for a friend.)
  • Time often goes first; topic before subject; verb always last.
  • Drop anything obvious from context.

22 Decision tree for endings

  1. Who am I talking to? → pick speech level (해요 vs 합니다 vs 반말).
  2. When? → past 았/었, present 아/어, future ~(으)ㄹ 거예요 / ~겠.
  3. Honor the subject? → insert ~(으)시~.
  4. What's the stem's last vowel? → choose 아 vs 어.
  5. Does the stem end in a 받침? → choose 은/는, 이/가, 을/를, 으- vs no-으-. Usually for smoother sound.
  6. Is the verb irregular? → apply the irregular rule.
  7. Connecting clauses? → pick the connector by nuance (서 vs 니까, 지만 vs 는데).
  8. Modifying a noun? → pick the modifier ending by tense + verb type.

24 Quick Study Tips

  • Learn 받침-vs-vowel pairs together (은/는, 이/가, 을/를, (으)면, (으)니까…). The pattern is identical, just one rule.
  • Don't translate "to be" — sometimes 이다, sometimes 있다, often nothing (adjectives conjugate themselves).
  • Particles ≠ word order, but particles + word order together carry meaning. Don't drop both.
  • Listen for sentence endings — politeness, mood, evidentiality all live there.
  • Embrace 는데 — it's the duct tape of Korean conversation.
